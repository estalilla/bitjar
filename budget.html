Basics of Budgeting

  • Intro
  • Examples
  • myBudget
'Never spend your money before you have earned it' - Thomas Jefferson

The first step in taking control of your finances is to assess just what exactly your finances are. Best way to do that is to create a budget. We'll go step-by-step and construct your own personal budget shortly, but first let's go over some basics.

Necessary vs. Discretionary Spending (or Need vs. Want)

Certain expenses are unavoidable. For example, you need food and water. Both cost money (unless you are a farmer or some sort of hunter/gatherer) and spending money of healthy food can have positive effects for you. Other basic needs cost money as well. The largest recurring expenses tend to be food and shelter (such as rent or mortgage payments). A few other examples include clothing, transportation, and trips to the doctor.

Then there is the stuff you want, but can still live without. These are discretionary expenses because you can decide to whether to buy them or not at any point. Examples such as designer clothes, new cars, or even your iPhone are luxuries (although some may argue that their smartphone is practically a vital appendage by now). These are not bad things to purchase, but it's important that you first make sure you can afford to cover your basic needs.

Earn up here, Live down here

Most people are aware that they shouldn't always splurge on items they can't afford. Yet why is it that so many people end up in debt, even those who make millions of dollars? One common explanation is that as people earn more money, their spending rises to meet their new level of wealth. This is called the income effect. Think back to when you were younger; you probably didn't spend as much money as you do now. Remember how you could easily get by on $50/week back in college? You probably spend that on food in a day now that you're workingfull-time. The income effect helps explain why even some seemingly wealthy people claim to not feel rich at all. The key takeaway is to live below your means and a budget will help highlight areas where you are spending more than you're capable of sustaining.

Assets and Liabilities

You can think of your budget as your personal balance sheet. A balance sheet is a snapshot of what a business owns and owes. Items on this list fall into one of two categories: asset or liability.

An asset is something that you own outright. They are expected to be beneficial, meaning that the hope is the asset will help generate income at some point. An example of an asset is rental property, which can be leased out to earn a monthly income.

Liablities refer to something that you owe, such as a loan. Liabilities can be helpful, such as when trying to purchase a house or car. For the most part, you will want to limit your liabilities to a manageable amount since you are on the hook for paying it back, even if your situation changes.

'Something poignant' - ME

If you work in the United States you will be paying a portion of your monthly paycheck to the government in the form of several types of taxes. Federal taxes are generally the largest depending your total income, followed by social security and state taxes (for states that have income taxes). Finally, Medicare and other health insurance payments may be taken out of your paycheck before you even see any of the money.
